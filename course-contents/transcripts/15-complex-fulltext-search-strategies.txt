Configuring stop words for text search

Full-text search in Redis performs natural language search. So for example, if you are looking for the term "amaze," if you have the term "amazing" in a document, the match will occur and the document will be returned. So text searches are based on the meaning of a word. In every language, there are certain words that are considered stop words. These are words that are filtered out before indexing your documents for search. Stop words are words that don't really add meaning to your text. Let's understand what I mean. Now, in the multi_fields index, in the description field, I'm going to search for the word "duck." This returns two documents in the search results. Now, if you look at the description for both of these documents, you'll find several occurrences of the word "the." I've highlighted a few of the occurrences of "the" here on screen. Now, "the" is a stop word in the English language because it doesn't really add much meaning. And if you search for stop words using RediSearch, you won't get any results. For example, here in the description field, I search for the term "the" which is a stop word, and you can see that even though the description of the records that we have seen contained the term "the," no results are returned for the search. Now, let's search our documents for two more terms that are not stop words, duck and racing. I've used the OR operator, the pipe, so I'm looking for documents with the term "duck" or "racing" in the description field. And you can see that there are several documents here in the result, a total of four search results. Now, let's perform a search for several different stop words. We know that these stop words do exist in the description of our video games. For|is|and|by all of these are stop words. I'm looking for them in the description field, but I get absolutely no results. These are words which have been filtered out of the index. Now, it's possible for you to configure your own stop words in an index. They may be terms that you consider stop words, that is, they add no meaning, but are not really stop words in the English language. Here, I create a new index called idx:vg:stop_word, and I specify two stop words. The term "duck" and "racing" are both stop words in this index. Under index, the description field has text for all of the documents with the prefix vg_sales:. Now in this index, both terms "duck" and "racing" will be considered stop words. You can confirm this by running an FT.INFO command on this stop word index. At the very bottom here, notice the stop words list that is created. Notice the terms "duck" and "racing" are present in there. These are our custom stop words. Now, if you perform a search for terms that are not in the stop word list, the search will work perfectly. So here I look for the terms "monster" or "puzzle" in the description field of my documents, and I get a few records that match these terms, monster or puzzle. But let's say I want to look for the terms "duck" or "racing." Remember, these are configured as stop words in the stop word index. Well, I'll get no documents in the result. These are stop words and have been filtered out of the index. You can search using these stop words.




Performing fuzzy search

Redis also supports fuzzy searches or approximate searches. Let me show you how fuzzy search works. I'm going to search for the term "sports" in the name index, that is, in the name of all of the documents that I have indexed. There are three documents that match my query and contain the term "sports" in the name. Let's say while typing out the term "sports," I misspelled it by mistake. I am searching for "spots" when I actually meant "sports." Now this is not a fuzzy search. The term "spots" is not present in the name of any document and I get zero results for my query. In order to account for spelling mistakes, I might want to perform a fuzzy search or approximate search. Now I search for "spots," but because I've specified the percentage signs, this search will be a fuzzy search and this will match the term "sports" that is close to "spots." Note that in the query I've enclosed the word "spots" in a single pair of percentage signs. This returns matches for words which are at a levenshtein distance of one from the term that I have specified. One way to think about it is that sports is just one character different from spots. Let me perform a search with a single pair of percentage signs once again. I'm looking for the word "thrift" for any term. This is a fuzzy search for any term that is at a levenshtein distance of one from the term "thrift." There are absolutely no matches here within a levenshtein distance of one. But let's say I were to enclose the term "thrift" in two sets of percentage signs that I have done here. This will search for approximate matches within a levenshtein distance of two, roughly words that can differ by two characters from the word "thrift." And this time around you see that there are multiple matches. The word "theft" is at a levenshtein distance of two from the word "thrift," and those matches were found.




Grouping and aggregation

RediSearch allows you to perform a search and then perform a grouping and aggregation operation on the results of the search. So not only will you get the search results, but you can also perform count and sum operations on numeric fields that are part of your search results. Grouping and aggregation operations are performed using the FT .AGGREGATE keyword. Notice I've invoked FT .AGGREGATE and then the index I've used is the idx: vg: multi_fields Index. My search here is just a wildcard search. It will match all documents and I group by one field, that is, the platform field. I've only grouped the data and I've not aggregated the data. This will give me all of the unique platforms for the different video games that exist in my Redis database. You can see that our video games run on eight different unique platforms. Now I'm performing grouping and aggregation using the wildcard search, but you can specify any search term you want as well. For example, here I search for the term "sports" and I group by platform. The search will be performed first, and a grouping will be performed on the search results, which means I'll get all of the unique platforms which contain sports related video games. There are just three unique platforms that match this query. Next, I'll perform a wildcard search and a simple grouping and count aggregation. Notice the asterisk or the star that is the wildcard search, I group by the platform field once again, and the reduce count keywords will count the number of video games that I have for each unique platform. Here you can see that for Xbox 360, there is one game and somewhere here for the Wii platform we have seven games. In the next query, I'm going to perform an aggregation, but no grouping. Again, you can see the star for the wildcard search. Notice my group by zero. This indicates that I'm not grouping by any field in my document. Instead, I use reduce count distinct to count the number of unique platforms that are present across all documents in my Redis server. And there are eight distinct platforms. So if you want an aggregation to be performed across your entire data and you're not grouping by any field, make sure you specify GROUPBY zero. When you use an index for a search operation and then a GROUPBY, you cannot group by a field that is not present in your index. For example, here the genre field is not part of my multi_fields index and this query results in an error. Similarly, if I try to perform an aggregation operation using a field that's not in the index, that's not going to work as well. For example, here the global sales field at global sales is not present in our multi_fields index. If I try to run this aggregation, you'll get an error that indicates that this aggregation on global sales cannot be performed using this particular index. I'll quickly set up a new index that does include the global sales field. I use FT .CREATE. The name of the index is idx: vg: agg_idx. Note the schema, the fields that are indexed are platform, genre, global sales. And on this particular index that does include global sales, I should be able to perform an aggregation on global sales using FT .AGGREGATE. I perform a wildcard search. Notice the asterisk or the star I GROUPBY the platform field and I perform a reduced sum on global sales. That is, I'm computing the total sum of sales based on platform. And here is a result, for every platform we get the sum of sales. Take a look at Number 6, For Wii, the total sum of sales is about $253.92 billion.
